# Capability-First Blog Playbook

**Purpose.** A repeatable content strategy for RelayKit blog and Indie Hackers posts that lead with a product capability the reader wants — not with SMS, compliance, or RelayKit. The capability is the hero. SMS is the enabling technology. RelayKit is the late reveal.

Pairs with the relaykit-writing-prose skill. That skill owns voice, prose craft, AI-tell avoidance, the canonical 5-beat structure (§4), the title formula (§5), the two lanes (§6), and the Indie Hackers surface rules (§10). This playbook does not repeat any of it. Read the skill first; use this for what to write and how to frame it.

**What's new here vs. the skill.** The skill already names the demand lane — "you have a problem you didn't know SMS could solve" — and targets ~60% of the corpus to it. This playbook makes that lane operational pre-launch, when RelayKit has no case studies of its own. New material: the cold-start research engine (§3), the capability→category→headline mapping (§4), and the borrowed-credibility anti-pattern (§5).

## 1. The principle: the capability is the hero

Nobody wakes up wanting SMS. They wake up wanting a better product. Most SMS companies open with "Need SMS?" / "Need A2P 10DLC compliance?" — and lose everyone who isn't already shopping.

Lead with the capability instead:

- Not "here's a communication channel" → but "here's something your app could now do."
- The reader thinks "oh — my app should probably do that" before they think about how.
- SMS is revealed as the enabling tech only after the outcome is sold. RelayKit is revealed last, as the thing that removes the reason most developers never build it.

Developers don't buy databases because they love databases — they buy "my app can now store customer data." They don't buy Stripe because they love payment processors — they buy "my app can now take money." RelayKit posts position SMS the same way: "my app can now do this."

This is the skill's demand lane (§6) made concrete. The post starts inside the reader's product; SMS arrives at the end as a resolution.

## 2. The capability-first beat sequence

A demand-lane application of the skill's canonical 5-beat (skill §4). Map onto it — don't replace it:

1. **The capability** — open on what the app could do, often as a question: "What if your app could rescue abandoned bookings before they vanish?" (skill beat: Hook)
2. **Why it matters** — the concrete cost of not having it: revenue that leaks, users who churn, support load. (Cost)
3. **Why most developers don't build it** — they assume it's simple, then hit carrier registration, consent, opt-out handling. The friction beat. (Misdiagnosis)
4. **How SMS enables it** — SMS is the unusually good fit: it gets read, and read fast. The outcome is now believable. (Insight)
5. **How RelayKit makes SMS sane** — soft, late. RelayKit removed the painful part. (Outward turn)

Keep beat 5 soft, per the skill — the demand lane plants a seed, it doesn't close.

## 3. Cold-start research engine (pre-launch)

The strategic core, and the part the skill doesn't cover.

Pre-launch, you are not proving that RelayKit works. You're proving the capability is valuable. Those are different bars, and the second is far easier — nobody expects a pre-launch product to have case studies, but everyone will read and share a striking industry outcome.

So the proof comes from synthesized industry knowledge, not RelayKit data you don't have yet:

- SMS reminders cut no-shows in appointment-based businesses.
- Recovery texts complete more abandoned bookings.
- Text verification lifts account activation.
- SMS open/read rates dwarf email.

The reader's reaction shifts from "who are these RelayKit people?" to "huh — I didn't know that." That is the whole move.

### The honesty rule (non-negotiable)

This only works if it reads as synthesis, not borrowed credibility — and only if the facts are real. Indie Hackers is in active backlash against inflated or fake metrics (skill §10):

- Every stat traces to a real, verifiable source. Cite it.
- Never invent a number. Never round a vague claim for effect ("a few thousand," "95%+") without a source behind it — that applies even to attractive headline numbers.
- Synthesize a pattern across several real sources; don't lean on one study as a crutch.
- If you can't source it, cut it. An honest "consistently shown across appointment-based businesses" beats a fabricated precise percentage.

## 4. One capability, one outcome, one category

Each post does exactly one of each. This is the spotlight-category discipline.

| Category | Capability / outcome | Example angles |
|----------|----------------------|----------------|
| Appointments | Recover no-shows and late cancellations | "The Most Valuable Feature Most Scheduling Apps Never Build" · "Why No-Shows Are Usually a Messaging Problem" |
| Waitlists | Fill last-minute openings automatically | "Why Restaurants Text and Everyone Else Emails" · "The Fastest Way to Fill a Last-Minute Opening" |
| Verification | Users actually receive the code | "The Password Reset Problem Nobody Talks About" · "The Feature Users Only Notice When It's Missing" |
| Orders | Customers know where their order is | "Why Customers Think Their Order Never Shipped" · "The Cheapest Support Ticket You'll Ever Eliminate" |
| Community | Reach members on the channel they read | "The Attention Channel Community Apps Forgot About" |
| Team alerts | Get the urgent thing seen now | "Email Is Too Slow for Some Problems" |

Two headline modes:

- **"What if your app could ___?"** — aspirational, curiosity-first. Strong for the demand lane.
- **"The [feature / problem] ___."** — declarative; names a recognizable product problem.

Title mechanics live in the skill (§5, §10): ~51 characters for IH, name a recognizable thing, frame as tension or surprise, never "I built" / "We launched." Any number in a title must be sourced (see §3).

## 5. The anti-pattern: borrowed credibility

The failure mode that kills these posts. Same facts, opposite outcome.

**Weak — borrowed credibility (nobody cares):**

> How RelayKit Helps Reduce No-Shows
> According to XYZ study… According to ABC study… According to DEF study… Use RelayKit.

Leads with RelayKit, stacks citations as a wall, makes RelayKit the subject. Reads as an ad.

**Strong — synthesis (RelayKit is the punchline):**

> The Most Valuable Feature Most Scheduling Apps Never Build
> OpenTable sends reminders. Calendly sends reminders. Mindbody sends reminders. Every serious scheduling platform eventually discovers the same thing: people forget — and when they forget, the business loses money. [Then: why it works, when it works, where it breaks down.] Building it yourself sounds simple, until you hit carrier registration, consent requirements, opt-out handling, and content rules. That's the problem RelayKit exists to solve.

RelayKit is the punchline, not the subject. The capability carried the entire post; RelayKit only closes it.

This also satisfies the skill's craft principle §8.4 (describe the ecosystem generously) and §8.3 (the friction must be real, or the post doesn't motivate the product).

## 6. Pre-publish checklist (layer on the skill's drop-in test)

- [ ] The hero is a capability / outcome — not SMS, not compliance, not RelayKit.
- [ ] SMS appears only after the outcome is sold; RelayKit only at the end, soft.
- [ ] Exactly one capability, one outcome, one category.
- [ ] Every stat is real and sourced. No invented or vaguely-rounded numbers.
- [ ] The friction is real, not a rhetorical scapegoat — the post still motivates RelayKit (skill §8.3).
- [ ] Passes the skill's drop-in test (§2) and AI-tell pass (§7).
- [ ] Title names a recognizable product problem, not an SMS feature.

## Why this works (keep in view)

- These are product posts, not SMS posts — what Indie Hackers actually reads. The feed is saturated with databases, auth providers, AI wrappers, analytics, and email tools; a business-outcome post stands out.
- They age well. "How RelayKit Works" goes stale fast; "The Most Valuable Feature Most Scheduling Apps Never Build" can pull traffic for years.
- They create demand instead of capturing it. Compliance content only reaches people already shopping for SMS. Capability content makes a much larger audience realize their app should do this — and the compliance story then becomes the natural second act: "I'd love that capability" → "right, SMS is a pain" → "oh — RelayKit already handled the painful part."
